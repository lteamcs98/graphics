Introduction

Why Computer Science?

One reason to take CS 1 is that it gives you your TLA distrib, so that you get a TAS and a lab course. Another reason is the cool professor. Yet another reason is that you'll get to write computer programs that do fun things.

You might be wondering whether Computer Science is right for you. This course will help you answer that question.

Careers

Maybe you would consider majoring in Computer Science, but you're concerned that the only career option open to CS majors leads to a Dilbert-like job. Not true!!! For one thing, there are many CS jobs that bear no resemblence to Dilbert's existence. Have you heard of a company called Google? They offer free meals—good food, I've tried it—to their employees. Not exactly a Dilbert-like experience.

Being a CS major opens up possibilites beyond programming jobs. Many CS majors have gone on to medical school, law school, and business school. One of my college roommates—a CS major—is a successful marketing consultant. Another, Dr. Michael First, is editor of the DSM-IV, the standard manual of psychiatric diagnosis, as well as other well-known psychiatry books. Another, Tom Hahn, is a programmer, but definitely not a Dilbert: he works at Pixar, and he has an Academy Award. Dartmouth CS majors have also gone into areas such as teaching and finance.

Consider a modified major with CS as part of it. Dartmouth students have created modified majors with CS and Engineering, Physics, and Math. Those are no surprise. How about Economics and Geography? Still not a big surprise? OK, how about Philosophy, Music, Film Studies, and Studio Art? Yup, each of these has been done! Even Art History!

Perhaps it's too late for you to start on a CS major or modified major. But it might not be too late to start on a CS minor. In fact, we had two ’99s who took CS 5 (a previous incarnation of our introductory course) in their junior spring and managed to complete CS minors. One of them was a Classics major, but with her CS minor, she landed a job at Oracle. The other was a Math major who got a Master's degree in CS right here and is now at Cray, a company that makes supercomputers.

You might also consider a minor in Digital Arts or Computational Methods, or even a major of CS modified with Digital Arts. CS 1 serves as one of the header courses for Digital Arts and Computational Methods.

I am always happy to talk to you about your career options. So is , the Undergraduate Adviser in the Computer Science Department.

Computer science is artistic

Computer science no more about computers than biology is about microscopes, astronomy is about telescopes, or creative writing is about pens or Microsoft Word.

Computer science is about solving problems, preferably in a beautiful and artistic way. Computers are used in every discipline from medicine (medical robotics, analysis of the spread of disease, design of drugs) to the visual arts; have you seen a Pixar film recently? Computer science is one of the newest disciplines; it has completely transformed our world and will continue to do so.

Above all else, computer science is a creative discipline, mixing some of the best aspects of engineering, art, science, language, and mathematics. Learning about computation will change how you view problems in every discipline, and how you think about solutions to those problems.

In class, I showed examples of some ideas in computer science, using this PowerPoint. We searched for who had my cheese, searched for my phone number in the phone book, and counted up how many people were present in class.

What is this course about?

You will write a lot of programs in this course. For the first four to five weeks of the term, we'll focus on learning the Javascript programming language. After that, we'll write larger and more exciting programs, but will also look at big ideas in computer science, using programming as a jumping-off point.

Some of the objectives are to learn how to do the following:

  1. Model problems for solution on a computer.
  2. Analyze the correctness, completeness, and efficiency of solutions to problems.
  3. Implement solutions, by writing good programs, with good design, structure, and style.

Why Javascript?

Javascript shares many similarities with other programming languages, including Java and C++, and once you've learned Javascript, these other languages would not be at all difficult to pick up. Javascript is a fairly popular language, and it's also fairly easy to get started with.

Functions. Coding style.

A computer program is made up of instructions that a computer follows. Let's get started.

Printing to the screen

print("Hello");

This program causes the computer to print the word Hello in the console window at the bottom of the page. Notice that the quotation marks around Hello are not printed.

Here's another program:

print("Hello");
print("my");
print("name");
print("is");
print("Inigo");
print("Montoya");

In this multi-line program, commands are executed in the order they appear.

Thinking like a computer: the program counter

As the Javascript interpreter runs a program, the interpreter keeps track of the line number that is currently being executed, much as you might use a bookmark to keep track of what page you're reading in a book. The bookmark that the computer uses to keep track of the line number is called the program counter.

The program counter always starts out pointing at the first line of the code in the program you run. It executes that line of code. Depending on what that line of code says, one of two things happens:

  1. The program counter increases by one and the interpreter executes the next line of code,
  2. OR the line of code instructs the interpreter to jump to some other line of code, and it sets the program counter appropriately.

Why would we ever want the interpreter to suddenly jump to a new line of code? We'll see many reasons, but the first is to write and use our own commands, called functions, that are made up of a chunk of lines of code.

By giving a name to a chunk of lines of code, we can easily reuse that section of code without having to retype all those lines every time.

Writing your own functions

Here's a program that defines our own two functions, say_introduction and threaten_vengeance.

function say_introduction() {
    print("My name is Inigo Montoya.");
}

function threaten_vengeance(){
    print("You killed my father.");
    print("Prepare to die.");
}

print("Hello.");

Function definitions have two parts: a header, and a body. The header gives the name of the function you want to define (as well as some other things we'll see later).

The header uses function, the name of the function you'd like to define, parentheses after the name of the function, and a left curly brace. function is an example of a keyword in Javascript: a word that has special meaning in a program. Keywords in Javascript may not be used for any other purpose; for example, you cannot give a function the name function.

So the header for the first function is

function say_introduction() {

The body of the first function is

print("My name is Inigo Montoya.");

When we run the complete program, we get the output:

Hello.

Wait. Our program only printed Hello.. Javascript now knows how to do the say_introduction and threaten_vengeance functions, but we haven't told it to use them yet.

To tell the interpreter to actually run the body of a function, you use a function call. To write a function call, write the name of the function followed by parentheses.

function say_introduction() {
print("My name is Inigo Montoya.");
}

function threaten_vengeance() {
    print("You killed my father.");
    print("Prepare to die.");
}

print("Hello.");
say_introduction();
threaten_vengeance();
print("Hello.");
threaten_vengeance();

Here's the output:

Hello.
My name is Inigo Montoya.
You killed my father.
Prepare to die.
Hello.
You killed my father.
Prepare to die.

One way to think of the difference between a function definition and a function call is to think of a cookbook containing various recipes. The printed recipes are like function definitions: they tell how to prepare a dish when you decide to prepare it, but they don't, on their own, cause the dish to be prepared. Actually preparing a dish by following the recipe is like a function call.

When the program counter reaches a function call, the interpreter takes a note of where the program counter is, moves the program counter to the first line of the body of the appropriate function definition, and executes the lines of code in the function. When the interpreter gets to the end of the body of the function, the program counter is reset to its original value before the function call, and Javascript jumps back to where it started before the function call.
